<p> <a>thumb|right|The &quot;phoenix&quot; sculpture exposed at the university entrance</a> The <strong>Universit&#x00e9; de Caen Basse-Normandie</strong> (or <strong>Caen University</strong>) is a <a>university</a> in <a>Caen</a>, in <a>France</a>. </p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p>The institution was founded in 1432 It originally consisted of a faculty of <a>Canon Law</a> and a faculty of <a>Law</a>. By 1438, it already had five faculties. The foundation was confirmed by the King of France <a>Charles VII the Victorious</a> in 1452.</p>
<p>On July 7, 1944, the university was totally destroyed by aerial bombing. Reconstruction began in 1948, the new university was inaugurated on June 1 and 2, 1957. Its logo, the mythical <a>Phoenix</a>, symbolises this revival.</p>
<h2>Miscellaneous</h2>
<ul>
  <li>The mathematician <a>Pierre Varignon</a>, whose work would influence the young <a>Leonhard Euler</a>, earned his M.A. from Caen in 1682.</li>
  <li><a>Pierre-Simon Laplace</a> (1749-1827) was introduced to mathematics in Caen by Christophe Gadbled and Pierre Le Canu. </li>
  <li><a>Henri Poincar&#x00e9;</a> (1854-1912) taught there between 1879 and 1881.</li>
  <li>The University contains a famous scale model of Rome.</li>
  <li>Those intending to become <a>advocates</a> or <a>solicitor</a>s in <a>Guernsey</a> (or, until recently, <a>Jersey</a>) must complete three months' study of Norman law at Caen University (<em>Certificat d&#x2019;&#x00c9;tudes Juridiques Fran&#x00e7;aises et Normandes</em>) prior to being called to the Guernsey or Jersey Bar, respectively.</li>
</ul>
