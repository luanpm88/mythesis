<p>The <strong>University of Graz</strong> (<a>German</a>, <em>Karl-Franzens-Universit&#x00e4;t Graz</em>), a highly respected <a>university</a> located in <a>Graz</a>, <a>Austria</a>, is the third-largest and second-oldest university in Austria.</p>
<p>Karl-Franzens-Universit&#x00e4;t, also referred to as the University of Graz, is the city's oldest university, founded in 1585 by Archduke <a>Charles II of Austria</a>.
For most of its existence it was controlled by the Catholic Church, and was closed in 1782 by Emperor <a>Joseph II</a> in an attempt to gain state control over educational institutions.
Joseph II transformed it into a <em>lyceum</em> where civil servants and medical personnel were trained.
In 1827 it was re-instituted as a university by Emperor <a>Francis I</a>, thus gaining the name <em>Karl-Franzens-Universit&#x00e4;t</em>, meaning <em>Charles Francis University</em>.
Over 22,000 students are currently enrolled at this university.</p>
<p><a>Ludwig Boltzmann</a> was professor at the Karl-Franzens Universit&#x00e4;t twice (1869-1873 and 1876-1890) developing his statistical theory of heat.
Nobel Laureate <a>Otto Loewi</a> taught at the University of Graz from 1909 until 1938.
<a>Victor Franz Hess</a> (Nobel prize 1936) graduated in Graz and taught here (1920-1931, 1937-1938).
<a>Erwin Schr&#x00f6;dinger</a> was briefly chancellor of the University of Graz in 1936.</p>
<p>With the establishment of the Department for Slovene language and literature at the University of Graz, foundations were set for the Slovenian university-level <a>Slovene studies</a>.</p>
