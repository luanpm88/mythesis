<p></p>
<p> The <strong><em>Universit&#x00e9; libre de Bruxelles</em></strong> (or <strong>ULB</strong>) is a <a>French</a>-speaking <a>university</a> in <a>Brussels</a>, <a>Belgium</a>.
It has 21,000 students, 29% of whom come from abroad, and an equally cosmopolitan staff.
</p>
<h2>Name </h2>
<p><em>Universit&#x00e9; libre de Bruxelles</em> means &quot;Free University of Brussels&quot; but this translation is rarely used since its <a>Dutch</a>-speaking counterpart, the <a>Vrije Universiteit Brussel</a>, also has the same meaning.
Some facilities shared by both universities use the name &quot;Brussels Free Universities&quot;, abbreviated BFU (e.g. the Brussels Free Universities Computing Center, BFUCC [<a>http://www.vub.ac.be/BFUCC/</a>]).
<a>thumb|left|The main building on the Solbosch campus.</a></p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p>The history of the Universit&#x00e9; libre de Bruxelles (ULB) is closely linked with that of Belgium itself.
When the nine provinces that broke away from the Kingdom of the Netherlands formed the Belgian State in 1830, there were three state universities in the country: Ghent, Liege and Leuven.
Even though Brussels had been promoted to the rank of capital, it still had no university.</p>
<p>For this reason, in 1831 a group of leading Brussels Masonic figures in the fields of the arts, science and education set themselves the objective of creating a university for the city.
They had the choice between a state university and, failing that, a private institution, since the Belgian Constitution, the most liberal in Europe, allowed for this possibility.</p>
<p>Finding the financial burden of the three existing universities too onerous, the Belgian government showed little enthusiasm for yet another state university.
However, when in 1834 the episcopate decided to found the <a>Catholic University of Mechlin</a>, things began to happen very quickly.
The liberal professions and Freemasons, lead by <a>Pierre-Th&#x00e9;odore Verhaegen</a> and <a>Auguste Baron</a>, who were promoting the Brussels university project, stepped up their efforts, with the result that the Free University of Belgium, as it was originally known, inaugurated its first academic year on 20 November 1834.</p>
<p>From 1836 it was to be called the Universit&#x00e9; Libre de Bruxelles, but although the geographical term may have changed, the adjective &quot;free&quot; remained.
This was a key point.
[<a>http://www.ulb.ac.be/docs/ulb-prestige/histuk.html</a>]</p>
<p>The school's <a>football (soccer)</a> team won the bronze medal at the <a>1900 Summer Olympics</a>.</p>
<p>Pierre-Th&#x00e9;odore Verhaegen, who helped make the university, is the symbol of the creation of the university.
November 20, called '<a>St V</a>', is a holiday for students of both the Universit&#x00e9; Libre de Belgique and the <a>Vrije Universiteit Brussel</a>.</p>
<p>Since 1935 some courses have been taught in both French and Dutch, but it was only in 1963 that all faculties held courses in both languages.
Shortly after the language dispute at the <a>Catholic University of Leuven</a>, in October 1969 the French and Dutch entities of the ULB separated into two distinct universities.
With the act of 28 May 1970, the Vrije Universiteit Brussel and the Universit&#x00e9; Libre de Bruxelles officially became two separate legal, administrative and scientific entities.

<h2>Campi</h2>
<p>The ULB comprises three main <a>campi</a>: the <em>Campus de la Plaine</em> in <a>Ixelles</a>, the <em>Campus du Solbosch</em>, on the territories of <a>Brussels</a> and <a>Ixelles</a> municipalities, in the <a>Brussels-Capital Region</a> and the <em>Campus Erasme</em> (faculty of medicine) in <a>Anderlecht</a> beside the <a>Erasmus Hospital</a> but the university also has buildings and activities in <a>Charleroi</a>, <a>Parentville</a>, <a>Treignes</a> and <a>Nivelles</a>.
