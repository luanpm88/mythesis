<p> <strong>Florida Agricultural and Mechanical University</strong>, commonly known as <strong>Florida A&amp;M</strong> or <strong>FAMU</strong>, is a <a>historically black university</a> located in <a>Tallahassee, Florida</a>, <a>United States</a>, the state capital, and is one of eleven member institutions of the <a>State University System of Florida</a>.
FAMU is also one of Florida's <a>land grant universities</a>.</p>
<h2>Academics</h2>
<p>FAMU has eight fully-funded endowed eminent scholars chairs including two in School of Journalism and Graphic Communications, four in the School of Business &amp; Industry, one in the College of Education, one in Arts and Sciences, and one in its School of Pharmacy.</p>
<p>The university offers 62 bachelor's degrees in 103 majors/tracks.
36 master's degrees with 56 majors/tracks are offered within eleven of the university's 13 schools and colleges.
Two professional degrees and eleven PhD degree programs are offered.</p>
<h3>Accreditation</h3>
<p>Florida A&amp;M University is accredited by Southern Association of Colleges and Schools (SACS) since 1935.
On June 21, 2007, the Commission on Colleges of the <a>Southern Association of Colleges and Schools</a> placed the university on probation for six months because the university failed to comply with several core requirements.
The university was removed from probation on June 26, 2008.</p>
<h3>College of Law</h3>
<p> On December 21, 1949, a division of law was established at the then Florida A&amp;M College and the first class was admitted in 1951.
The legislature established the school because no &quot;separate but equal&quot; state-supported law school existed for African-Americans at that time.
The school's enrollment was limited to African- American male students and was located in Tallahassee, Florida.
Seventy-seven percent of the entering class are Florida residents, and 42% are non-minority students.
The mean LSAT score of the 2009 entering class was 146.</p>
<h3>College of Pharmacy and Pharmaceutical Sciences</h3>
<p>The School of Pharmacy was organized in 1951.
It received its present name in 1985 in recognition of the expanded role and mission of the college in professional and graduate education.
It is now one of the largest colleges of pharmacy in the country.
The Pharmacy School in 2009&#x2013;2010 graduate student enrollment is 122, with 42 PhDs, 21 DrPH, 45 MPH, and 14 MS candidates.
The school has graduated over 60% of African-American PhDs in pharmaceutical sciences, since 1990.
It is accredited by the Accreditation Council for Pharmacy Education (ACPE) through June 30, 2010.</p>
<h3>Research</h3>
<p>FAMU&#x2019;s annual research funding currently exceeds $54 million.
Research is funded by grants from the <a>National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>, the <a>National Science Foundation</a> and the <a>National Institutes of Health</a>.
For 2007-2008, the largest source of funds was $15.1 million from the US Department of Education, followed by $6.1 million from the Department of Agriculture (most of which is allocated to FAMU by virtue of it being a <a>land grant university</a>.)
FAMU's two largest research areas are agriculture and heath sciences.</p>

<h2>Student life</h2>
<h3>Demographics</h3>
<p>Florida A&amp;M University student enrollment population consists primarily of undergraduates.
Ninety percent of the schools enrolled students are <a>African-American</a>.
The next largest demographic group is <a>White (non-Hispanic)</a> students at 5%. <a>Native Americans</a>, <a>Hispanics</a> and <a>Asian Americans</a> round out the remaining 5%.

<h3>Athletics</h3>
<p>  Florida A&amp;M University is a member of the <a>Mid-Eastern Athletic Conference</a> and participates in <a>NCAA</a> <a>Division I-AA</a>.
FAMU's sports teams are called the &quot;Rattlers.&quot;
FAMU offers men's sports in baseball, basketball, football, golf, swimming , tennis and track and field.
It offers women's sports in basketball bowling, softball, swimming, tennis, track and field and volleyball.</p>
<p>From 1938 to 1961, the football team won the Black College National Championship eight times, including six times under head coach <a>Jake Gaither</a>, in 1950, 1952, 1954, 1957, 1959 and 1961.
When Gaither retired after 25 years of coaching in 1969, his FAMU teams had a 203-36-4 (wins-losses-ties) record, for a .844
winning percentage.
Thirty-six players from Gaither's teams were <a>All-America</a>ns, and 42 went on to play in the <a>National Football League</a>.
During his 25 years as head coach, FAMU won 22 <a>Southern Intercollegiate Athletic Conference</a> championships.
Gaither was elected to the <a>College Football Hall of Fame</a> in 1975.
FAMU went on to win the first NCAA D1-AA National Championship in 1978 after defeating the <a>University of Massachusetts</a>.
The Rattlers meet the <a>Bethune-Cookman Wildcats</a> every year in the <a>Florida Classic</a> on the third weekend in November.
The Rattlers lead the overall series with Bethune Cookman University, 45-15-1.
One of the most notable wins in FAMU football history came when they defeated the <a>University of Miami</a> in 1979.</p>
<p>On November 15, 2008, Florida A&amp;M football received national attention when ESPN's <a>College GameDay</a> was broadcast live from the campus.
FAMU became the first historically black college or university campus and is one of two FCS schools to ever host the program (the other being the <a>University of Pennsylvania</a>).</p>
<p>The men's basketball team has twice qualified for the opening round game of the NCAA men's basketball tournament (2004 &amp; 2007).</p>
<h3>Marching band</h3>
<p> The FAMU Marching &quot;100&quot; under the direction of Dr. William P. Foster, was invited by the French government to participate in the Bastille Day Parade as the official representation from the United States.
This event was held in celebration of the bicentennial of the French Revolution.</p>
<p>The Marching &quot;100&quot; was named the &quot;Best Marching Band in the Nation&quot; by Sports Illustrated (August 1992).
The band received national recognition in January 1993 when it performed in the 52nd Presidential Inauguration Parade by invitation of <a>Bill Clinton</a>.
The band has also performed in the Super Bowl and in the 56th Presidential Inauguration Parade.</p>
