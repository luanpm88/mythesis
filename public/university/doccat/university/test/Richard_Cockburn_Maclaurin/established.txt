<p> <strong>Richard Cockburn Maclaurin</strong> ( &quot;Co-burn&quot;) (June 5, 1870 &ndash; January 15, 1920), was a <a>Scottish</a>-born <a>U.S.</a> educator and mathematical <a>physicist</a>.
He was made president of <a>MIT</a> in 1909, and held the position until his death in 1920.</p>
<p>During his tenure as president of MIT, the Institute moved across the <a>Charles River</a> from <a>Boston</a> to its present campus in <a>Cambridge</a>.
<p>Earlier, he was a foundation professor of the then <a>Victoria College</a> of the <a>University of New Zealand</a> from 1899 to 1907.
A collection of lecture theartres at the Kelburn campus of that university were named after him.
He was also a professor at <a>Columbia University</a> from 1907 to 1908.</p>
<p>Maclaurin was born in Scotland, and was related to the noted Scottish mathematician <a>Colin Maclaurin</a>.
He came out to New Zealand with his family at the age of four.
His brother <a>James Scott Maclaurin</a> (1864-1939) was a noted chemist, who invented the Cyanide process for extracting gold.</p>
