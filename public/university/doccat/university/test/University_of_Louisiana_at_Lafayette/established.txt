It is the largest campus within the eight-campus <a>University of Louisiana System</a> and is the second largest university in the state of Louisiana.</p>
<p>Founded in 1898 as an industrial institute, the university developed into a four-year college in the 1920s and became known by its present name in 1999.
Concurrently the university evolved into a major national research and doctoral university as noted by its <a>Carnegie</a> categorization as a RU/H: research university (high research activity), among the top 5% of all U.S. colleges.
It offers Louisiana's only PhD in <a>francophone studies</a> and the only <a>industrial design</a> degree, and only doctoral <a>cognitive science</a> program in the U.S. South.
The university has achieved several milestones in <a>computer science</a>, <a>engineering</a> and <a>architecture</a>.
It is also home to a distinct College of the Arts.</p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p><a>right|thumb|270px|One of the numerous &quot;Century Oaks&quot; planted on the campus in 1900.</a></p>
<li>1901 - SLII opened Sept. 18 with 100 students and eight faculty members.</li>
<li>1903 - 18 students were the first to graduate from SLII.</li>
<li>1920 - Began a four-year course culminating with a bachelor of arts degree.</li>
<li>1921 - SLII was changed into the <strong>Southwestern Louisiana Institute</strong> (SLI).</li>
<li>1960 - SLI became the <strong>University of Southwestern Louisiana</strong> (USL).</li>
<li>1984 - USL attempted to change its name to the <a>University of Louisiana</a>, which only lasted a few days until overturned by a district court.</li>
<li>1999 - USL was renamed the <strong>University of Louisiana at Lafayette</strong>.</li>
<li>1954 - Within months of the <em><a>Brown v. Board of Education</a></em> decision by the <a>U.S. Supreme Court</a>, SLI admitted 70 African American students, to become the first all white college in the <a>Deep South</a> to achieve racial <a>desegregation</a>.</li>
<li>1962 - Offered the first <a>master of science</a> degree in <a>computer science</a> in the U.S.</li>
<li>2007 - The Cajun Advanced Picosatellite Experiment (CAPE) successfully launches the State of Louisiana's first university student built satellite.</li>
<p><a>left|thumb|220px|Martin Hall, named for late state Sen. Robert Martin, who passed the bill founding the institution.</a>
;Campus:UL Lafayette's <a>campus</a> consists of 137 <a>acre</a>s (554,000&nbsp;m&#x00b2;) lined by <a>live oak</a> trees planted in 1900.
Its <a>quadrangle</a> is encircled by a &quot;Walk of Honor&quot; path which contains more than 80,000 bricks bearing the names of every graduate, beginning with the first graduating class of 1903.</p>
<p>Also centered in the main campus is <a>Cypress Lake</a>, a swamp-like microcosm of the nearby <a>Atchafalaya Basin</a>, home to alligators, turtles, birds and fish.</p>
The complex also includes <a>Cajun Field</a>, <a>Blackham Coliseum</a>, several other athletic facilities for training and competitions, fraternity and sorority rows, and the physical education and recreational facilities.</p>
The nearby Center for Ecology and Environmental Technology has 51 acres (206,000&nbsp;m&#x00b2;).</p>
<p>;Renewable resources labs:The university has a  <a>farm</a>/<a>renewable resources</a> laboratory with a  pond for <a>crawfish</a> and <a>catfish</a> culture in Cade, Louisiana.
The university also has a horse farm in the center of <a>Lafayette</a> and several other farmlands around <a>Acadiana</a>.</p>
<p><a>right|thumb|250px|The Paul and Lulu Hilliard University Art Museum.</a>
<p>The Paul and Lulu Hilliard University Art Museum permanent collection consists of more than 1,500 works of art, including paintings, prints, drawings, sculptures and photographs.
This collection represents 18th, 19th and 20th century <a>Louisiana</a>, as well as the United States, <a>Europe</a> and <a>Japan</a>.</p>
KRVS currently broadcasts to 651,000 residents in the <a>Acadiana</a> area of <a>Louisiana</a>.
KRVS also broadcasts live from <a>Festival International</a> every April in support of local art and music.</p>
<p>The university is a member of the <a>Southeastern Universities Research Association</a> and is categorized as a <a>Carnegie</a> RU/H: Research University (high research activity).
The university was rated one of the top 100 public research universities in the nation according to a 2010 report by The Nelson A. Rockefeller Institute of Government.</p>
<p>In 2006 the university inaugurated the $29M technology initiative LITE Center, which offers the largest known three-dimensional-immersive auditorium for visualization of fully interactive <a>3D model</a>s and data sets for seismic analysis, computer-aided modeling, product stress test analysis and a host of other applications that require visualization of large data sets for scientists.
The center collaborates with students in the <a>architecture</a>, <a>engineering</a>, <a>computer science</a> fields as well as others, allowing use of its facilities to increase local business and furthering of the technological economy in Lafayette.

<p><strong><em>Louisiana History</em></strong></p>
<p>The journal <em>Louisiana History</em> is published quarterly through UL Lafayette by the <a>Louisiana Historical Association</a>.
LHA was founded in <a>New Orleans</a> in 1889.</p>
<p>In 2004, UL Lafayette students and faculty produced <a>CajunBot</a>, one of 25 autonomous vehicles that competed in the <a>U.S.
Department of Defense</a> 2004 &amp; 2005 <a>DARPA Grand Challenge</a>s as well as the 2007 <a>DARPA Urban Challenge</a>.
CajunBot, which was featured on <a>CNN</a> and on the <a>Discovery Channel</a> science series <a>Robocars</a>, used <a>artificial intelligence</a> and <a>GPS</a> positioning to navigate a designated route while detecting and avoiding obstacles.</p>
<p>A group of UL Lafayette engineering students participating in the <a>Cajun Advanced Picosatellite Experiment (CAPE)</a> built a small artificial <a>satellite</a>, known as a <a>CubeSat</a>, that was launched into orbit from the <a>Republic of Kazakhstan</a> in 2007.</p>
<p>In January 2008, The UL Lafayette School of Architecture Design was selected to participate in the 2009 Solar Decathlon.
The entry of the Louisiana Solar Home named <a>BeauSoleil</a> took first place in market viability and was awarded the &quot;People's Choice&quot;.</p>
<p><strong><em>Vermilion</em> Newspaper</strong>&lt;br/&gt; UL Lafayette publishes a weekly newspaper, established in 1904, named the <a><em>Vermilion</em> Newspaper </a>.
The newspaper has a running tradition of publishing an <em>April Fool's</em> edition in which humorous and parody stories are run as an <a>Onion</a>-like satire.
The <em>Vermilion</em> has earned many <a>SPJ</a> journalist awards for published work since its inception.</p>
<p>The <em>Vermilion</em> has changed formats many times in its inception, ranging from a magazine cover and layout, to a traditional newspaper format.
It has also run various in-house projects chronicling the night life and cultural events in Lafayette in an attempt to connect students with the local culture.</p>
<p><strong><em>L'Acadien</em> Yearbook</strong>&lt;br/&gt; Instituted in the first years of the University, <em>L'Acadien</em> yearbook is an annual publication chronicling the events and student life on campus.
The Yearbook is staffed by students with a faculty oversight.
