<p>He was born in <a>New York City</a>, graduated at <a>Yale</a> in 1820,  spent a year in legal study in <a>Philadelphia</a>, and two years of the study of <a>theology</a> at <a>Princeton</a>.
For some time, he was a tutor at Yale, then went abroad to study Greek in <a>Leipzig</a>, <a>Bonn</a>, and <a>Berlin</a>.
From 1831 to 1846 he was <a>professor</a> of Greek at Yale.
After being chosen as president of Yale, he instructed students of history, <a>political economy</a>, <a>political science</a>, and especially international law.
He resigned as president of Yale in 1871.</p>
<p>During his 25 years as president, Yale advanced in wealth and influence and two new departments, the <a>Scientific School</a> and the <a>School of Fine Arts</a>, were begun.
Woolsey was one of the founders of the <em>New Englander</em>, chairman of the American commission for the revision of the Authorized Version of the Bible, president of the World's Evangelical Alliance at its international meeting in New York, a lifelong member and at one time president of the <a>American Oriental Society</a>, and a regent of the <a>Smithsonian Institution</a>.
Among his writings and publications are these:  Editions of the <em>Alcestis</em> of <a>Euripides</a> (1834), of the <em>Antigone</em> of <a>Sophocles</a> (1835), of the <em>Prometheus</em> of [[&#198;schylus]] (1837), of the <em>Electra</em> of Sophocles (1837), and of the <em>Gorgias</em> of <a>Plato</a> (1843); an edition of <a>Lieber</a>'s <em>Civil liberty and Self Government</em>, and:</p>
<li><em>Introduction to the study of International Law</em> (1860, many times republished)</li>
<li><em>Essays on Divorce and Divorce Legislation</em> (1869)</li>
<li><em>Religion of the Present and Future</em>, a collections of sermons (1871)</li>
Woolsey Street in <a>New Haven, Connecticut</a> is also named in his honor.
Theodore Dwight Woolsey's daughter Elizabeth Dwight Woolsey married as his second wife <a>Daniel Coit Gilman</a>, the Yale-educated first president of <a>Johns Hopkins University</a>.</p>
<p>The statue erected in his memory has a golden toe from being rubbed for good luck.</p>
