It consistently ranks among the best universities in <a>Northern Europe</a> in international rankings and is generally considered one of the most prestigious institutions of higher learning in <a>Europe</a>.</p>
It shares some peculiarities, such as the <a>student nation</a> system, with <a>Lund University</a> and the <a>University of Helsinki</a>.</p>
It has a teaching staff of 4,000 (part-time and full-time) out of a total of 6,000 employees.
<p>Architecturally, Uppsala University has traditionally had a strong presence in the area around <a>the cathedral</a> on the western side of the <a>River Fyris</a>.
The <a>archbishopric of Uppsala</a> had been one of the most important <a>sees</a> in <a>Sweden proper</a> since Christianity first spread to this region in the ninth century.
Uppsala had also long been a hub for regional trade, and had contained settlements dating back into the deep Middle Ages.
As was also the case with most medieval universities, Uppsala had initially been chartered through a <a>papal bull</a>.
This included the right to establish the four traditional faculties of <a>theology</a>, <a>law</a> (<a>Canon Law</a> and <a>Roman law</a>), <a>medicine</a>, and <a>philosophy</a>, and to award the bachelors, masters, licentiate, and doctorate degrees.
There is some evidence of academic studies in Uppsala during the 16th century; the Faculty of Theology is mentioned in a document from 1526, King <a>Eric XIV</a> appointed <a>Laurentius Petri Gothus</a> (later archbishop) rector of the university in 1566, and his successor and brother <a>John III</a> appointed a number of professors in the period 1569&#x2013;1574.
Of the professors, several were taken over from the <a>Collegium Regium</a> in Stockholm, which had been functioning for a few years but closed in 1593.

<p>As the dominant academic institution in Sweden for several centuries, Uppsala University has ever since its first period of expansion in the early part of the 17th century educated a large proportion of Swedish politicians and civil servants, from 17th century Chancellor of the Realm (<em>rikskansler</em>) <a>Johan Oxenstierna</a> (1611&#x2013;1657) and Lord Chief Justice (<em>riksdrots</em>) <a>Magnus Gabriel De la Gardie</a> (1622&#x2013;1686) to the first Social Democratic <a>Prime Minister of Sweden</a>, <a>Hjalmar Branting</a> (1860&#x2013;1925) and many later politicians.
Other alumni are <a>Dag Hammarskj&#x00f6;ld</a> (1905&#x2013;1961), <a>UN</a> Secretary General who was (posthumously) awarded the <a>Nobel Peace Prize</a> in 1961, and the Swedish diplomat <a>Hans Blix</a> (b.
1928), who was Head of the <a>International Atomic Energy Agency</a> 1981-1997, of the <a>UNMOVIC</a> 2000-2003, and previously Swedish Minister of Foreign Affairs 1978&#x2013;1979.
<p>Most Swedish clergymen, including most bishops and archbishops, have been educated at the university, including, in more recent times, <a>Nathan S&#x00f6;derblom</a> (1866&#x2013;1931), Professor of the History of Religions in the Faculty of Theology, later <a>Archbishop of Uppsala</a>, and awarded the <a>Nobel Peace Prize</a> in 1930 for his work as leader of the <a>ecumenical</a> movement.</p>
<p>The university became prominent in the sciences in the 18th century with names such as the physician and botanist <a>Carolus Linnaeus</a> (1707&#x2013;1778), the father of <a>taxonomy</a>, and his numerous important pupils, the physicist and astronomer <a>Anders Celsius</a> (1701&#x2013;1744), inventor of the centigrade scale the predecessor of the Celsius scale, and the chemist <a>Torbern Bergman</a> (1735&#x2013;1784).
Another scientist from this era is <a>Emanuel Swedenborg</a> (1688&#x2013;1772), better remembered today as a religious mystic.
Uppsala scientists of the 19th century include the physicist <a>Anders Jonas &#x00c5;ngstr&#x00f6;m</a> (1814&#x2013;1874).
<p>Many well-known Swedish writers have studied in Uppsala: <a>Georg Stiernhielm</a> (1698&#x2013;1672) is often called the father of Swedish poetry.
The writer, historian and composer <a>Erik Gustaf Geijer</a> (1783&#x2013;1847), professor of history, and the poet <a>Per Daniel Amadeus Atterbom</a> (1790&#x2013;1855), professor of poetry, were principal figures of early 19th century Swedish romanticism.
Other Uppsala alumni are the poet <a>Erik Axel Karlfeldt</a> (1864&#x2013;1931), who refused the <a>Nobel Prize for Literature</a> in 1918, but received it posthumously in 1931, the novelist and playwright <a>P&#x00e4;r Lagerkvist</a> (1891&#x2013;1974), <a>Nobel laureate</a> in 1951, and the poet and novelist <a>Karin Boye</a> (1900&#x2013;1941), for whom one branch of the university library has been named.
The <a>Communist</a> leader <a>Ture Nerman</a> (1886&#x2013;1969) wrote a novel called <em>Olympen</em>, based on his experience as a student in Uppsala.
<a>Niklas Zennstr&#x00f6;m</a>, co-founder of <a>KaZaA</a> and <a>Skype</a> is also a former student at Uppsala University.
<p>Together with <a>Lund</a>, Uppsala is the historic and traditional centre of Swedish academic life, making it a popular object of reference in Swedish literature, art, and film.
