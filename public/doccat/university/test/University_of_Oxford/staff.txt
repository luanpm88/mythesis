<p>   The <strong>University of Oxford</strong> (informally <strong>Oxford University</strong>, or simply <strong>Oxford</strong>) is a <a>university</a> located in <a>Oxford</a>, <a>United Kingdom</a>.
In addition to cultural and practical associations as a historic part of British society, the two universities have a long <a>history of rivalry</a> with each other.</p>
<a>League tables</a> consistently list Oxford as one of the UK's best universities, and Oxford consistently <a>ranks</a> in the world's top 10.</p>
<p>For more than a century, it has served as the home of the <a>Rhodes Scholarship</a>, which brings students from a number of countries to study at Oxford as postgraduates or a second bachelor's degree.
<p>The University is a member of the <a>Russell Group</a> of research-led <a>British universities</a>, the <a>Coimbra Group</a>, the <a>League of European Research Universities</a>, <a>International Alliance of Research Universities</a> and is also a core member of the <a>Europaeum</a>.
It forms part of the <a>'Golden Triangle'</a> of British universities and is a constituent of the <a>'G5'</a>.</p>
<a>Lady Margaret Hall</a> (1878) was followed by <a>Somerville College</a> in 1879; the first 21 students from Somerville and LMH attended lectures in rooms above an Oxford baker's shop.),
<a>St Hilda's</a> (1893) and <a>St Anne's College</a> (1952).
<p>As a <a>collegiate university</a>, Oxford's structure can be confusing to those unfamiliar with it.
The university is a <a>federation</a>: it comprises over forty self-governing <a>colleges</a> and <a>halls</a>, along with a central administration headed by the <a>Vice-Chancellor</a>.
Departments provide facilities for teaching and research, determine the syllabuses and guidelines for the teaching of students, perform research, and deliver lectures and seminars.
Colleges arrange the tutorial teaching for their undergraduates.

Five Pro-Vice-Chancellors have specific responsibilities for Education; Research; Planning and Resources; Development and External Affairs; and Personnel and Equal Opportunities.
Colleges have responsibility for admitting undergraduates and organising their tuition; for graduates, this responsibility falls upon the departments.</p>
The passing of two sets of examinations is a prerequisite for a first degree.
All MAs were members of Convocation and until 1913 all resident members of <a>Convocation</a> were members of <a>Congregation</a>.
MAs, as members of Convocation, elected the <a>Chancellor</a> and <a>Professor of Poetry</a>, but recently Convocation has been widened to consist of all graduates.</p>

<p>Within these terms, Council determines for each year eight-week periods called <a>Full Term</a>s, during which undergraduate teaching takes place.
For the University, key sources were <a>HEFCE</a> (&#x00a3;166m) and research grants (&#x00a3;213m).
For the colleges, the largest single source was endowments and interest (&#x00a3;82m) and residential charges (&#x00a3;47m).
Prospective students apply through the <a>UCAS</a> application system, in common with most British universities, but (along with applicants for <a>Cambridge</a>) must observe an earlier deadline.
Because of the high volume of applications and the direct involvement of the faculty in admissions, students are not permitted to apply to both Oxford and Cambridge in the same year, with the exception of applicants for <a>Organ Scholarships</a> and those applying to read for a second undergraduate degree.</p>
<a>St Hugh's College</a>, for example, states that it accepts graduate students in most subjects, principally those in the fields of interest of the Fellows of the college.
Oxbridge entrance remains a central focus for many private and selective-state schools, and the lack of a more representative social mix at the university remains a point of national controversy.
<p>Students who apply from <a>state school</a>s and colleges have a comparable acceptance rate to those from <a>independent school</a>s (25% and 32% of applicants accepted respectively, 2006).

<p>Mature and part-time students are supported by the <a>Oxford University Department for Continuing Education</a>.</p>
With a total possible grant of &#x00a3;10,235 over a 3-year degree, it is the most generous <a>bursary</a> scheme offered by any British university.
<p>Students successful in early examinations are rewarded by their colleges with <a>scholarship</a>s and <a>exhibitions</a>, normally the result of a long-standing endowment, although when tuition fees were first abolished, the amounts of money available became purely nominal.
<p>From the inception of the <a>Church of England</a> until <a>1866</a> membership of the church was a requirement to receive the <a>BA degree</a> from Oxford, and &quot;dissenters&quot; were only permitted to receive the MA in 1871.
Knowledge of <a>Ancient Greek</a> was required until 1920, and Latin until 1960.
[[Radcliffe Camera]], built 1737-1749, holds books from the <a>Bodleian Library</a>'s <a>English</a>, <a>History</a>, and <a>Theology</a> collections.]]</p>
Oxford's central research library is the <a>Bodleian</a>, founded by <a>Sir Thomas Bodley</a> in 1598 and opened in 1602.
Its main central site consists of the <a>Radcliffe Camera</a>, the Old Schools Quadrangle, the <a>Clarendon Building</a>, and the New Bodleian Building.
A tunnel underneath <a>Broad Street</a> connects the buildings.
There are plans to build a new book depository in Osney Mead, and to remodel the New Bodleian building  to better showcase the library&#x2019;s various treasures (which include a Shakespeare <a>First Folio</a> and a <a>Gutenberg Bible</a>) as well as temporary exhibitions.
Several other libraries, such as the <a>Bodleian Law Library</a>, <a>Indian Institute Library</a>, <a>Radcliffe Science Library</a> and the <a>Oriental Institute Library</a>, also fall within the Bodleian&#x2019;s remit.</p>
It holds significant collections of art and archaeology, including works by <a>Michelangelo</a>, <a>Leonardo da Vinci</a>, <a>Turner</a>, and <a>Picasso</a>, as well as treasures such as the <a>Scorpion Macehead</a>, the <a>Parian Marble</a> and the <a>Alfred Jewel</a>.
It is housed in a large neo-Gothic building on <a>Parks Road</a>, in the University&#x2019;s <a>Science Area</a>.
Among its collection are the skeletons of a <em><a>Tyrannosaurus rex</a></em> and <em><a>triceratops</a></em>, and the most complete remains of a <a>dodo</a> found anywhere in the world.
It also hosts the <a>Simonyi</a> <a>Professorship of the Public Understanding of Science</a>, currently held by <a>Marcus du Sautoy</a>.</p>
In the Faculty of Music on <a>St Aldate's</a> is the <a>Bate Collection</a> of Musical Instruments, a collection mostly comprising of instruments from Western classical music, from the medieval period onwards.
<a>Christ Church Picture Gallery</a> holds a collection of over 200 <a>old master</a> paintings.
Oxford is ranked first in Politics, Physiological Sciences, English, Fine Art, <a>Business Studies</a>, <a>Middle East</a>ern and <a>African Studies</a>, Music, Philosophy, and also Education and Linguistics which it shares first with Cambridge.
Oxford comes second after Cambridge in a further seventeen subjects.
<p>In the Guardian's subject tables for institutions in tariff-band 6 (universities whose prospective students are expected to score 400 or more tariff points) Oxford took first place for Anatomy and Physiology, Anthropology, Biosciences, Business and <a>Management Studies</a>, <a>Earth</a> and <a>Marine Sciences</a>, <a>Economics</a>, English, Law, Materials and Mineral Engineering, <a>Modern Languages</a>, Music, Politics, Psychology, and Sociology.
Oxford came second to Cambridge in Geography, Archaeology, Classics, History, <a>History of Art</a>, Mathematics, Philosophy, Theology and <a>Religious Studies</a>.
In the 2010 <a>QS World University Rankings</a> Oxford University dropped a place to sixth in the world (while <a>Cambridge University</a> came first), falling from fifth (alongside <a>Imperial College London</a>) in the 2009 <a>THE - QS World University Rankings</a> (in 2010 <a>Times Higher Education World University Rankings</a> and <a>QS World University Rankings</a> parted ways to produce separate rankings).
<p>Twenty-six British <a>prime ministers</a> have attended Oxford, including <a>William Gladstone</a>, <a>Herbert Asquith</a>, <a>Clement Attlee</a>, <a>Harold Macmillan</a>, <a>Harold Wilson</a>, <a>Edward Heath</a>, <a>Margaret Thatcher</a>, <a>Tony Blair</a> and most recently <a>David Cameron</a>.</p>
This number includes <a>Harald V of Norway</a>, <a>Abdullah II of Jordan</a>, two <a>Prime Ministers of Canada</a> (<a>Lester B. Pearson</a>, and <a>John Turner</a>), two <a>Prime Ministers of India</a> (<a>Manmohan Singh</a> and <a>Indira Gandhi</a>), four <a>Prime Ministers of Pakistan</a> (<a>Liaquat Ali Khan</a>, <a>Huseyn Shaheed Suhrawardy</a>, <a>Zulfiqar Ali Bhutto</a>, and <a>Benazir Bhutto</a>), <a>Eric Williams</a> (Prime Minister of Trinidad and Tobago), <a>&#x00c1;lvaro Uribe</a> (Colombia's former President), <a>Abhisit Vejjajiva</a> (Prime Minister of Thailand) and <a>Bill Clinton</a> (the first <a>President of the United States</a> to have attended Oxford; he attended as a <a>Rhodes Scholar</a>).
<a>Arthur Mutambara</a> (Deputy Prime Minister of <a>Zimbabwe</a> ),was a <a>Rhodes Scholar</a> in 1991.
The Burmese democracy activist and <a>Nobel laureate</a>, <a>Aung San Suu Kyi</a>, was a student of St. Hugh's College.
Another religious figure was <a>Mirza Nasir Ahmad</a>, the third <a>Caliph</a> of the <a>Ahmadiyya Muslim Community</a> and <a>Shoghi Effendi</a>, one of the appointed leaders of the <a>Baha'i faith</a>.
Some fifty Olympic medal-winners have academic connections with the university, including <a>Sir Matthew Pinsent</a>, quadruple gold-medallist rower.
<a>T.
E. Lawrence</a> was a student at <a>Jesus College</a>, while other illustrious students include the explorer, courtier, and man of letters, <a>Sir Walter Raleigh</a>, (who attended <a>Oriel College</a> but left without taking a degree) to the Australian media oligarch, <a>Rupert Murdoch</a>.
The founder of <a>Methodism</a>, <a>John Wesley</a>, studied at Christ Church and was elected a fellow of <a>Lincoln College</a>.</p>
<p>The long list of writers associated with Oxford includes <a>John Fowles</a>, <a>Theodor Geisel</a>, <a>Thomas Middleton</a>, <a>Samuel Johnson</a>, <a>Robert Graves</a>, <a>Evelyn Waugh</a>, <a>Lewis Carroll</a>, <a>Aldous Huxley</a>, <a>Oscar Wilde</a>, <a>C.

E. Housman</a>, <a>W.
H. Auden</a>, <a>T.
S. Eliot</a>, <a>Wendy Perriam</a> and <a>Philip Larkin</a>, and seven <a>poets laureate</a> (<a>Thomas Warton</a>, <a>Henry James Pye</a>, <a>Robert Southey</a>, <a>Robert Bridges</a>, <a>Cecil Day-Lewis</a>, <a>Sir John Betjeman</a>, and <a>Andrew Motion</a>).</p>
<p>Economists <a>Adam Smith</a>, <a>Alfred Marshall</a>, <a>E.
F. Schumacher</a> and <a>Amartya Sen</a>, and philosophers, <a>John Locke</a>, <a>Thomas Hobbes</a> and <a>Jeremy Bentham</a>, have all spent time at Oxford, as have major scientific pioneers such as <a>Albert Einstein</a> and <a>Erwin Schr&#x00f6;dinger</a>.
Some pioneers of the <a>scientific revolution</a>, such as <a>Robert Hooke</a> and <a>Robert Boyle</a>, either studied at Oxford or were otherwise associated with the university.</p>
<p>Some contemporary scientists include <a>Stephen Hawking</a>, Nobel prize-winner <a>Anthony James Leggett</a>, and <a>Tim Berners-Lee</a>, <a>Kate Beckinsale</a>, <a>Michael Palin</a>, were undergraduates at the University, as were <a>Oscar</a>-winner <a>Florian Henckel von Donnersmarck</a> and <a>Richard Curtis</a>.
Sportspeople who have attended the university include <a>Imran Khan</a>.
