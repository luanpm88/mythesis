guarding the entrance to the courtyard]] The <strong>Royal Institute of Technology</strong> (, abbreviated <strong>KTH</strong>) is a <a>university</a> in <a>Stockholm</a>, <a>Sweden</a>.
Today KTH institutions and faculties are distributed across several campuses in <a>Stockholm County</a>, located in <a>Flemingsberg</a>, <a>Haninge</a>, <a>Kista</a> and <a>S&#x00f6;dert&#x00e4;lje</a> in addition to the ones in &#x00d6;stermalm.</p>
<p>KTH's earliest Swedish predecessor was the <em>Laboratorium mechanicum</em>, a collection of mechanical models for teaching created in 1697 by <a>Christopher Polhem</a>, who is considered to be the father of <a>mechanics</a> in Sweden.
<p>The institute had one professor of <a>chemistry</a> and one of <a>physics</a>, and one class in <a>mechanical engineering</a> and one in <a>chemical engineering</a>.
Instead, Joachim &#x00c5;kerman, the head of the School of Mining in <a>Falun</a> and a former professor of chemistry at KTH, took over.
An entrance test and a minimum age of 16 for students was introduced, which led to the creation of a proper engineering training at the institute.
In 1863 the institute received its own purpose-built buildings on <a>Drottninggatan</a>.
For a while, the title <em>civilingenj&#x00f6;r</em> was equal to &quot;KTH graduate&quot; but in 1937, <a>Chalmers</a> in <a>Gothenburg</a> became the second Swedish engineering college which were allowed to confirm these titles.</p>
With the mission to &quot;make something with neutrons&quot;, the Swedish team, with scientists like <a>Rolf Maximilian Sievert</a>, set out to research the subject and eventually build a <a>nuclear reactor</a> for testing.</p>
Today this might seem ill-considered, since approximately 40,000 people lived within a 1&nbsp;km radius.
<p>At 18:59, 13 July 1954, the reactor reached <a>critical mass</a> and sustained Sweden's first nuclear reaction.
