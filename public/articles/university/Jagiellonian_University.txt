<p>The <strong>Jagiellonian University</strong> (, often shortened to <em>UJ</em>; historical names: , University of Krakow, Krak&#x00f3;w Academy, The Main Crown School, Main School of Krak&#x00f3;w) was established in 1364 by <a>Casimir III the Great</a> in <a>Krak&#x00f3;w</a>. It is the oldest university in <a>Poland</a>. It is the second oldest university in <a>Central Europe</a> and one of the <a>oldest universities in the world</a>. </p>
<p>In 1817 it was renamed <em>Jagiellonian University</em> to commemorate Poland's <a>Jagiellonian dynasty</a>, which had revived the University of Krakow after it had fallen upon hard times.&lt;ref name=&quot;officialhistory&quot;&gt;&lt;/ref&gt; In 2006 <em><a>The Times Higher Education Supplement</a></em> ranked Jagiellonian University as the top Polish university.&lt;ref&gt;Times Higher Education Supplement (THES)   Rank 287 worldwide as the first Polish university listed among the top 500 in 2006.&lt;/ref&gt;</p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p>Poland's King <a>Casimir III</a> realized that the nation needed a class of educated people, especially lawyers, who could codify the country's laws and administer the courts and offices.  His  efforts to found an institution of higher learning in Poland were rewarded when <a>Pope Urban V</a> granted him permission to open the University of Krakow. The Royal Charter of Foundation was issued on 12 May 1364, and a simultaneous document was issued by the City Council granting privileges to the <a>Studium Generale</a>.  The King provided funding for one chair in <a>liberal arts</a>, two in <a>Medicine</a>, three in <a>Canon Law</a> and five in <a>Roman Law</a>, funded by a quarterly payment taken from the proceeds of the royal monopoly on the salt mines at <a>Wieliczka</a>.&lt;ref&gt;&lt;/ref&gt;  Its development was stalled by the death of the king, and later the academy was re-established (1400) by King <a>W&#x0142;adys&#x0142;aw Jagie&#x0142;&#x0142;o</a> and his wife <a>Jadwiga</a>. The queen donated all of her personal <a>jewelry</a> to the university, allowing it to enroll 203 students.  The faculties of astronomy, law and theology attracted eminent scholars: for example, <a>Stanis&#x0142;aw of Skarbimierz</a>, <a>Pawe&#x0142; W&#x0142;odkowic</a>, <a>Jan of G&#x0142;og&#x00f3;w</a>, and <a>Albert Brudzewski</a>, who from 1491 to 1495 was one of <a>Nicolaus Copernicus's</a> teachers. Jagiellonian University was the first university in Europe to establish independent chairs in <a>Mathematics</a> and <a>Astronomy</a>.</p>
<p>[[File:Matejko UJ.JPG|thumb|left|upright|Founding of the University, painting by [[Jan Matejko]]]]</p>
<p>Throughout the history of the University, thousands of students from all over Poland, from <a>Lithuania</a>, <a>Russia</a>, <a>Hungary</a>, <a>Bohemia</a>, <a>Germany</a>, and <a>Spain</a> have studied there. In the second half of the 15th century, over 40% of  students came from countries other than the Kingdom of Poland. For several centuries, virtually the entire intellectual elite of Poland was educated at the University.</p>
<p>The first chancellor of the University was <a>Piotr Wysz</a> and the first professors were Czechs, Germans and Poles, many of them trained at the <a>Charles University</a> in <a>Prague</a> in <a>Bohemia</a>. By 1520 Greek philology was introduced by <a>Constanzo Claretti</a> and <a>Wenzel von Hirschberg</a>;  Hebrew was also taught. The Golden era of the University of Krakow took place during the <a>Polish Renaissance</a>, between 1500 and 1535, when it was attended by 3,215 students in the first decade of the 16th century. As the popularity declined, this record was not surpassed until the late 18th century.</p>

<p>In 1846, after the <a>Krak&#x00f3;w Uprising</a>, the city and its university became part of the <a>Austrian Empire</a>.&lt;ref name=&quot;officialhistory&quot;/&gt; The threat of a closure of the University was dissipated in 1847 by the <a>Austrian Emperor's</a> decree to maintain it. Buildings, including the <a>Collegium Novum</a> (opened in 1887), were added.&lt;ref name=&quot;officialhistory&quot;/&gt;</p>
<p>On November 6, 1939, 184 professors were arrested and deported to <a>Sachsenhausen concentration camp</a> during operation codenamed <a>Sonderaktion Krakau</a>. The university, along with the rest of Poland's higher and secondary education was shut down for the remainder of <a>World War II</a>.&lt;ref name=&quot;pp2bio&quot;&gt;&lt;/ref&gt; The faculty was also suppressed by the Communists in 1954.&lt;ref name=&quot;pp2bio&quot;/&gt;</p>
<p>Since 2000 a new complex of university buildings, the so-called Third Campus, is under construction, due for completion in 2010 (public funds earmarked for the project amount to PLN 946,5 mln, or EUR 240 mln).&lt;ref name=ThirdCampus&gt;&lt;/ref&gt; The Third Campus borders the LifeScience Park managed by the Jagiellonian Centre of Innovation. In 2007, the administrative offices including those of the Rector and Deans were located at the historic <a>Collegium Novum</a>.</p>

<h2>Enrollment</h2>
<p>The university has 52,445 students (including 1,612 degree students from abroad) and 3,657 academic staff (2008). About 1130 international non-degree students were enrolled in the year 2007. Programmes of study in 48 disciplines and 93 specialisations are offered.&lt;ref&gt;Newsletter, web: <a>UJ-News35-PDF</a>.&lt;/ref&gt; The school has an exchange program with <a>The Catholic University of America</a> and its <a>Columbus School of Law</a>.&lt;ref&gt;&lt;/ref&gt;  It also hosts a semester abroad program from University of Wisconsin - Steven's Point and the University of Guelph.</p>
<h2>Library</h2>
<p>The university's <a>Jagiellonian Library</a> (<em>Biblioteka Jagiello&#x0144;ska</em>) is one of the largest in the country, with almost 6.5 million volumes.&lt;ref&gt;&lt;/ref&gt;  It has a large collection of medieval manuscripts,&lt;ref&gt;&lt;/ref&gt;  for example <a>Copernicus'</a> <a><em>De Revolutionibus</em></a> or <a>Balthasar Behem Codex</a>. </p>
<p>It also gathered the underground literature (so called <em>drugi obieg</em> or <a>samizdat</a>) from the period of Communist rule (1945&#x2013;1989).</p>

<h2>Student life</h2>
<p>In 1851, the first student scientific association was founded. Now, over 70 student scientific associations exist at the Jagiellonian University. Usually, their purpose is to promote students' scientific achievements by organizing lecture sessions, science <a>excursion</a>s, even international conferences for students like the International Workshop for Young Mathematicians, organized by the <a>Zaremba</a> Association of Mathematicians.</p>
