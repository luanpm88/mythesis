<p></p>
<p><strong>Friedrich Schiller University of Jena</strong> (FSU) (<a>German</a> <strong><em>Friedrich-Schiller-Universit&#x00e4;t Jena</em></strong>, colloquially <strong><em>Uni Jena</em></strong>), is a <a>public</a> <a>research university</a> located in <a>Jena</a>, <a>Thuringia</a>, <a>Germany</a>.
</p>
<p>The university was established in 1558 and is counted among <a>the ten oldest universities in Germany</a>.
In 1934, the university was renamed after the writer <a>Friedrich Schiller</a> who has been teaching as professor of history when Jena attracted some of the most influential minds at the turn from the 18th to the 19th century.
With <a>Karl Leonhard Reinhold</a>, <a>Johann Gottlieb Fichte</a>, <a>G.
W. F. Hegel</a>, <a>F.
W. J. Schelling</a> and <a>Friedrich von Schlegel</a> on its teaching staff, the university has been at the centre of the emergence of <a>German idealism</a> and <a>early Romanticism</a>.
</p>
<p>As of 2009, the university has around 21,000 students enrolled and 340 professors.
Its current rector, <a>Klaus Dicke</a>, is the 317th rector in the history of the university.</p>

<h2>History</h2>
<p>[[File:Collegium Jenense.jpg|thumb|University of Jena around 1600.
Jena was the center of [[Gnesio-Lutheran]] activity during the controversies leading up to the <a>Formula of Concord</a>.]]
The elector <a>John Frederick, Elector of Saxony</a> first thought of a plan to establish a university at <a>Jena</a> in 1547 while he was being held captive by emperor <a>Charles V</a>.
The plan was put into motion by his three sons and, after having obtained a charter from the emperor <a>Ferdinand I</a>, the university was established on 2 February 1558.</p>
<p>Prior to the 20th century, University enrolment peaked in the 18th century.
The university's reputation peaked under the auspices of duke Charles Augustus, <a>Goethe</a>'s patron (1787&ndash;1806), when <a>Gottlieb Fichte</a>, <a>G.
W. F. Hegel</a>, <a>Friedrich Schelling</a>, <a>Friedrich von Schlegel</a> and <a>Friedrich Schiller</a> were on its teaching staff.<a>thumb|upright|left|Friedrich
Schiller.</a></p>
<p>Founded as a home for the new religious opinions of the sixteenth century, it has since been one of the most politically radical universities in Germany.
Jena was noted among other German universities at the time for allowing students to <a>duel</a> and to have a passion for <em>Freiheit</em>, which were popularly regarded as the necessary characteristics of German student life.
The University of Jena has preserved a historical detention room or <a>Karzer</a> with famous caricatures by Swiss painter <a>Martin Disteli</a>.</p>
<p>At the end of the 18th and the beginning of the 19th centuries, the German government militated against the university, which remained unpopular until recent times.
This is believed to have been caused by the opening of new universities and the suspicions of the various German governments in regard to the democratic ideas coming out of Jena.</p>
<p>In the latter 19th century, the department of <a>zoology</a> taught <a>evolutionary theory</a>, with <a>Carl Gegenbaur</a>, <a>Ernst Haeckel</a> and others publishing detailed theories at the time of <a>Darwin</a>'s <em>&quot;<a>Origin of Species</a>&quot;</em> (1858).
The later fame of <a>Ernst Haeckel</a> eclipsed Darwin in some <a>Europe</a>an countries, as the term &quot;<a>Haeckelism</a>&quot; was more common than <a>Darwinism</a>.
[[Image:Ernst Haeckel 2.jpg|thumb|upright|right|[[Ernst Haeckel]].]]</p>
<p>In 1905, Jena had 1,100 students enrolled and its teaching staff (including <em>privatdozenten</em>) numbered 112.
Amongst its numerous auxiliaries then were the library, with 200,000 volumes; the observatory; the meteorological institute; the botanical garden; the seminaries of theology, philology, and education; and the well-equipped clinical, anatomical, and physical institutes.</p>
<p>During the 20th century, the cooperation between <a>Zeiss</a> corporation and the university brought new prosperity and attention to Jena, resulting in a dramatic increase in funding and enrolment.</p>
<h2>Research</h2>
<p>Research at Friedrich Schiller University traditionally focusses on both humanities and sciences.
In addition to the faculties the following &quot;Collaborative Research Centres&quot; (<a>German</a> &quot;Sonderforschungsbereich&quot;, short: &quot;SFB&quot;) operate at the university:</p>
<ul>
  <li>SFB 630 &#x201e;Erkennung, Gewinnung und funktionale Analyse von Wirkstoffen gegen Infektionskrankheiten&#x201c;</li>
  <li>SFB/TR 18 &#x201e;Relativistische Laser-Plasma-Dynamik&#x201c;</li>
  <li>SFB 604 &#x201e;Multifunktionelle Signalproteine&#x201c;</li>
  <li>SFB 580 &#x201e;Gesellschaftliche Entwicklung nach dem Systemumbruch.
Diskontinuit&#x00e4;t, Tradition und Strukturenbildung&#x201c;</li>
  <li>SFB 482 &#x201e;Ereignis Weimar-Jena.
Kultur um 1800&#x201c;</li>
  <li>SFB 436 &#x201e;Metallvermittelte Reaktionen nach dem Vorbild der Natur&#x201c; </li>
</ul>
<p>In 2006 the research center &#x201e;Jena Center - History of the 20th century&#x201c; was founded.
In 2007 the graduate school 214 &#x201e;Jena School for Microbial Communication&#x201c; was established within the <a>German Universities Excellence Initiative</a>.
In 2008 the &quot;Center for Molecular Biomedicine&quot; (CMB) and the interdisciplinary research center &quot;Laboratory of the Enlightenment&quot; were developed as the university's youngest research institutions.
</p>
<p>Friedrich Schiller University is the only German University with a chair for <a>gravitational theory</a>.</p>
