<p>, abbreviated as , is a major <a>research university</a> located in <a>Tokyo</a>, <a>Japan</a>.
The University has 10 <a>faculties</a> with a total of around 30,000 students, 2,100 of whom are foreign.
Its five campuses are in <a>Hong&#x014d;</a>, <a>Komaba</a>, <a>Kashiwa</a>, <a>Shirokane</a> and <a>Nakano</a>.
It is considered to be the most prestigious university in Japan.
It ranks as the highest in Asia and 20th in the world in 2010 according to <a>Academic Ranking of World Universities</a>.</p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p>The university was founded by the <a>Meiji</a> government in 1877 under its current name by amalgamating older government schools for medicine and Western learning.
It was renamed &quot;the &quot; in 1886, and then  in 1887 when the <a>Imperial University</a> system was created.
In 1947, after Japan's defeat in <a>World War II</a>, it re-assumed its original name.
With the start of the new university system in 1949, <em>Todai</em> swallowed up the former First Higher School (today's Komaba campus) and the former Tokyo Higher School, which henceforth assumed the duty of teaching first and second-year undergraduates, while the faculties on Hongo main campus took care of third and fourth-year students.</p>
<p>Although the university was founded during the Meiji period, it has earlier roots in the Astronomy Agency (&#x5929;&#x6587;&#x65b9;; 1684), Shoheizaka Study Office (&#x660c;&#x5e73;&#x5742;&#x5b66;&#x554f;&#x6240;; 1797), and the Western Books Translation Agency (&#x8543;&#x66f8;&#x548c;&#x89e3;&#x5fa1;&#x7528;; 1811).
These institutions were government offices established by the &#x5fb3;&#x5ddd;&#x5e55;&#x5e9c; <a>Tokugawa</a> shogunate (1603&#x2013;1867), and played an important role in the importation and translation of books from Europe.
</p>
<p><a>Kikuchi Dairoku</a>, an important figure in Japanese education, served as president of Tokyo Imperial University.</p>
<p>For the <a>1964 Summer Olympics</a>, the university hosted the running portion of the <a>modern pentathlon</a> event.</p>

<h2>Campus</h2>
<h3>Hongo Campus</h3>
<p>The main Hongo campus occupies the former estate of the <a>Maeda</a> family, <a>Edo period</a> <a>feudal lord</a>s of <a>Kaga Province</a>.
The university's best known landmark, Akamon (the Red Gate), is a relic of this era.
The symbol of the university is the <a>ginkgo</a> leaf, from the trees found throughout the area.

<h4>Sanshiro Pond</h4>
<p><a>thumb|Sanshiro Pond detail, Tokyo University's Hong&#x014d; campus.</a></p>
<p>,  university's Hongo campus, dates to 1615.
After the fall of the <a>Osaka Castle</a>, the shogun gave this pond and its surrounding garden to <a>Maeda Toshitsune</a>.
With further development of the garden by <a>Maeda Tsunanori</a>, it became known as one of the most beautiful gardens in <a>Edo</a> (now <a>Tokyo</a>), with the traditional eight landscapes and eight borders, and known for originality in artificial pond, hills, and pavilions.
It was at that time known as <em>Ikutoku-en</em> (Garden of Teaching Virtue).
The pond's contours are in the shape of the character <em>kokoro</em> or <em>shin</em> (<a>heart</a>), and thus its official name is <em>Ikutoku-en Shinjiike</em>.
It has been commonly called Sanshiro Pond after the title of <a>Natsume S&#x014d;seki</a>'s novel <em>Sanshiro</em>.</p>
<h3>Komaba Campus</h3>
<p> One of the five campuses of the University of Tokyo, the Komaba Campus is home to the College of Arts and Sciences, the Graduate School of Arts and Sciences, the Graduate School of Mathematical Sciences, and a number of advanced research facilities and campus services.
This is the campus where all the freshmen and sophomores of the University of Tokyo spend their college life.
The University of Tokyo is the only university in Japan which has a system of two years of general education before students can choose and move on to special fields of study.
The Komaba Campus is the cornerstone of general education, and was designated as the &quot;center of excellence&quot; for three new areas of research by the Ministry of Education and Science.
There are currently over 7,000 students (freshmen and sophomores) enrolled in the general education courses, about 450 students (juniors and seniors) pursuing their specialties in the College of Arts and Sciences, and 1,400 graduate students in the advanced study.</p>
