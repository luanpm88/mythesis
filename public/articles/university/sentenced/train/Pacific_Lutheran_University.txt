<p>  <strong>Pacific Lutheran University</strong> (PLU) is located in <a>Parkland</a>, a suburb of <a>Tacoma, Washington</a>.
In September 2009, PLU had a student population of 3,582 and approximately 280 full-time faculty.
It currently consists of the College of Arts and Sciences (consisting of the Division of Humanities, the Division of Natural Sciences, and the Division of Social Sciences), the School of Arts and Communication, the School of Business, the School of Education and Movement Studies, and the School of Nursing.</p>
<h2>History</h2>
<p>Founded in 1890 by <a>Scandinavia</a>n, mostly <a>Norwegian</a>, immigrants to further Lutheran higher education in the <a>Pacific Northwest</a>, PLU is an institution of the <a>Evangelical Lutheran Church in America</a>.
The school merged with <a>Everett's</a> Columbia College in 1920, became a two-year school, and resumed operations as Pacific Lutheran College.
<a>Spokane</a> College merged with Pacific Lutheran College nine years later.
PLC resumed awarding four-year degrees in 1939.
Pacific Lutheran gained university status in 1960.
PLU retains strong ties with its Scandinavian heritage, having been visited multiple times by <a>Norwegian</a> and <a>Swedish</a> <a>monarchs</a> and the Norwegian Parliament, <a>Stortinget</a>.</p>
<h2>Academics</h2>
<p>The university music department includes touring ensembles, such as <a>Choir of the West</a>.
PLU has a reputation for diverse academic offerings including Schools of Business, Education and Nursing, as well as well-respected programs in Pre-Medicine and Pre-Law preparation.
Many students are involved in the arts, including music, theatre, and dance.
PLU's student media and organizations have yet to win any awards.</p>
<p>Prominently highlighted in all academic programs is the opportunity for international education.
International education is built into the curriculum.
PLU became the first American university to have students studying abroad on all seven continents at the same time  <a>150px|thumb|right</a></p>

<h2>Campus</h2>
<p>The campus of PLU is unofficially divided into two sections, Upper Campus and Lower Campus, with Hinderlie Hill dividing them.
Upper Campus is home to: </p>
<ul>
  <li>Harstad Hall, or &quot;Old Main&quot;, the oldest building on campus, once housed the whole university but is now used primarily as a residence hall.
It is an all-female hall, the only single sex hall on campus.</li>
<li>Phillip Hauge Administration Building</li>
  <li>University Center, which has recently been renovated to meet L.E.E.D. Certification SILVER standards.
This houses the all-campus cafeteria, called the University Commons, and the Old Main Market.
In addition it houses the offices of Campus Ministry, Student Involvement and Leadership (SIL), Residential Life, the Diversity Center, the Scandinavian Center, Student Media (KCNS, the Mast Newspaper, KCCR Student Radio, etc.), Resident Hall Association, the Associated Students of PLU (the student government usually referred to as ASPLU), Dining Services, Conferences and Events, and the Chris Knutzen Lecture Hall.</li>
  <li>Eastvold Chapel</li>
  <li>Mortvedt Library</li>
  <li>Xavier Hall, which houses the Philip Nordquist Lecture Hall</li>
  <li>Ramstad Hall</li>
  <li>Ingram Hall</li>
  <li>Mary Baker Russell Music Center and Lagerquist Concert Hall, which houses the Gottfried and Mary Fuchs Organ</li>
  <li>Stuen Hall </li>
  <li>Ordal Hall</li>
  <li>Kreidler Hall (Originally West Hall)</li>
  <li>Hinderlie Hall (Originally South Hall for Women) became the men's hall on campus, officially Hinderlie, but widely nicknamed after a local beer, &quot;Rainier&quot; or &quot;Rainier House&quot; from the 1970s to the early '90s.
&quot;Rainier&quot; housed primarily new students and members of the varsity football team in the 1980s.
Hinderlie became co-ed in 1993.
</li>
  <li>Hong International Hall (Originally North Hall) has five language wings (Norwegian, Chinese, French, German, and Spanish) and the International Honors wing.</li>
</ul>
