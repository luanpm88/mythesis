<p> <strong>Noah Porter, Jr.</strong> (December 14, 1811 &ndash; March 4, 1892) was an <a>American</a> academic, <a>philosopher</a>, <a>author</a>, <a>lexicographer</a> and President of <a>Yale College</a> (1871-1886).</p>
<h2>Biography</h2>
<p>He graduated from <a>Yale College</a> in 1831 and was ordained as a <a>Congregational</a> minister in <a>New Milford</a>, <a>Connecticut</a> from 1836 to 1843.
He served as pastor at a Congregational Church in <a>Springfield</a>, <a>Massachusetts</a> from 1843 to 1846.
He was elected professor of <a>moral philosophy</a> and <a>metaphysics</a> at Yale in 1846.</p>
<p>Porter was inaugurated as President of Yale College on Wednesday, October 11, 1871.
He continued to serve as head of the college until 1886 </p>
<p>Porter edited several editions of <a>Webster's Dictionary</a>, and wrote on education.
</p>
<p>Influenced by the German refugee writer and philosopher Francis Leiber, Porter opposed slavery and integrated an antislavery position with religious liberalism.</p>
<p>He was a frequent visitor to the <a>Adirondack Mountains</a> of <a>New York</a>, and in 1875 was among the first recorded to make an ascent of the peak later named <a>Porter Mountain</a> in his honor.
Noah Porter, Jr. was the son of Noah Porter, one of the first ministers of First Church of Christ, Congregational 1652 in <a>Farmington</a>, Connecticut, and was the older brother of <a>Sarah Porter</a>, founder of <a>Miss Porter's School</a>, a college preparatory school for girls.</p>
<p>His best-known work is <em>The Human Intellect</em>, with an <em>Introduction upon Psychology and the Human Soul</em> (1868), comprehending a general history of philosophy, and following in part the &quot;common-sense&quot; philosophy of the Scottish school, while accepting the <a>Kantian</a> doctrine of intuition, and declaring the notion of design to be <em><a>a priori</a></em>.
Of great importance were two other works, <em>Elements of Intellectual Science</em> (1871) and <em>Elements of Moral Science</em> (1885).
</p>
<p>He died in <a>New Haven</a>.</p>
